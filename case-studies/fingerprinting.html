Fingerprinting

  • 2026
TL;DR Process analysts used to spend close to two months manually piecing together how a department actually worked — tweak by tweak, person by person — before they could call it "the process." I turned a raw pattern-detection algorithm into a feature that does that in moments. Spot Award.

Context

A pattern-detection algorithm with no way to act on it

The starting point was an algorithm out of Pega's Process & Task Mining suite — it read screen-level activity data and flagged clusters of screens that looked like a task. That's all it did: groups of screens, no framing, no way for anyone to act on it.

Used by process analysts, ops managers, and sometimes department heads — anyone trying to understand how a department's workflow actually runs, versus how it's documented to run.

Problem

Two months of manual work, every time

Understanding a department's real process meant manual reconstruction: talking to people, watching how work actually got done, noticing each person's individual tweak on the "official" process, then working out the broad pattern underneath all those variations.

For a sizeable department, that could take close to two months. One process analyst told me directly it saved his team weeks.

Process

From raw output to something an analyst would trust

I was handed the algorithm's raw output — clusters of screens it flagged as a likely task, nothing else. The real design problem was turning that into something a process analyst would actually trust and act on: what to surface, how to frame a detected pattern, how much of the algorithm's reasoning to show versus hide.

I worked closely with the product manager and the architect who held the patent on the algorithm — brought designs to them, took feedback, iterated. The design team's reviews shaped a lot of the refinement too.

The core tension: show too little and the analyst can't judge whether to trust the suggestion. Show too much and the algorithm's complexity becomes their problem to manage. Progressive disclosure was the answer — surface the suggestion cleanly, let the detail unfold only when they ask for it.

Solution

Raw output → one clear suggestion

Before: a tangle of individual habits and workarounds across a whole department, manually cross-referenced by hand. After: the algorithm's output surfaced as a single, clear suggestion — ranked, in plain language, with reasoning available on request.

Outcome

Tested with 6 analysts. Spot Award.

Tested directly with 6 client-side process and senior analysts — each given the prototype and asked to complete tasks on their own, no guidance. From first concept to VP buy-in to client testing permission to running the sessions: about 2.5 months.

One participant didn't complete — a call-center analyst evaluating a tool built for process analysts. Different role, different mental model. That's noted plainly here rather than excluded from the count.

Spot Award. One process analyst told me directly it saved his team weeks.
